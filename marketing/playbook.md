# Fork in the Road — Marketing Playbook

## Purpose
This playbook gives Fork in the Road a practical marketing system that a small owner-operated shop can actually keep up with. The goal is not to be everywhere. The goal is to become easy to discover, easy to recommend, and easy to remember.

## Core Positioning
Fork in the Road should market itself as the local pizza place for people who want something more personal, more memorable, and more customizable than generic lunch options.

### Simple message
- Local, not corporate
- Unique, not boring
- Made-to-order, not one-size-fits-all
- Great for groups, visitors, and adventurous locals

A useful internal shorthand is: **not Panera, not boring**.

## Best-Fit Channels
Fork in the Road does not need a giant content machine. It needs a few high-leverage channels:
- Google Business Profile
- Instagram
- Direct outreach to hotels, workshop hosts, and business lunch coordinators
- One-page leave-behind materials for local partners
- Word-of-mouth fueled by memorable pizzas and community storytelling

## Google Business Profile Checklist
Google is one of the highest-value channels because it captures people already looking for somewhere to eat.

### Setup and optimize
- Claim and fully verify the listing
- Make sure business name, address, phone, hours, and website are accurate
- Add primary and secondary categories that clearly describe the shop
- Write a short, warm description using phrases like custom pizza, local spot, unique menu, and group-friendly lunch
- Upload strong real photos: storefront, slices, whole pies, custom orders, people enjoying food, inside atmosphere
- Keep hours updated, especially seasonal or holiday changes
- Add ordering or call links where appropriate

### Ongoing habits
- Post new photos regularly, especially unusual or request-based pizzas
- Ask happy customers for reviews in a natural way
- Reply to every review kindly and with personality
- Use review language as proof points in future marketing

### Review prompt script
“Thanks for coming in. If you enjoyed it, a Google review really helps a small local place like us.”

## Instagram Strategy
Instagram is the best simple storytelling channel because Fork in the Road already has naturally visual content.

### What to post
- Customer-requested pizzas
- Specials or unusual combinations
- Close-up food shots that feel real, not overproduced
- Behind-the-counter or making-of moments
- Group orders for workshops or events
- Community moments tied to the pay-it-forward program or neighborhood goodwill

### Content pillars
#### 1. Request-based pizza stories
Show the pizza and explain what made it special.

Example:
“Today’s custom request: hot honey, ricotta, basil, and a little risk. This one turned out beautiful.”

#### 2. Memorable lunch alternative
Show why this is better than generic catering.

Example:
“Feeding a workshop or office? We’re a lot more fun than the usual box lunch.”

#### 3. Human local business energy
Let people feel the shop’s personality.

Example:
“Built by real people, made to order, and always open to a good pizza idea.”

### Posting rhythm
- 2–3 feed posts per week
- 3–5 story posts per week if possible
- Repost customer photos whenever available and allowed

### Instagram rules
- Use real photos over polished graphics
- Keep captions short and warm
- End some posts with a light prompt, like “What should we try next?”
- Do not overdesign everything; authenticity is part of the appeal

## Hotel Partnership Pitch
Hotels need easy recommendations for guests who want something local and reliable.

### Goal
Become a front-desk favorite for guests asking where to grab lunch or an interesting local bite.

### What to offer
- Simple recommendation card or one-pager
- Clear explanation of what makes the shop distinctive
- Easy phone/order information
- Optional mention that custom requests are welcome

### Pitch angle
“This is a Portland pizza spot with personality — easy to recommend when guests want something more memorable than a chain.”

## B2B Outreach Template Guidance
Workshop hosts, office coordinators, and group organizers are ideal because they need food that is easy to order and enjoyable to talk about.

### What to emphasize
- Group-friendly ordering
- More memorable than standard lunch chains
- Flexible menu and request-taking
- Local business with real personality

### Tone
- Short
- Specific
- Low-pressure
- Useful, not salesy

## Travel Group / Tour Operator Outreach
Travel organizers care about smooth logistics, local flavor, and low friction.

### What to emphasize
- Easy group coordination
- Flexible order planning
- Local Portland feel
- Food that is more interesting than generic stopover meals

### Best use case
This channel matters most before and during peak visitor seasons, when travel planners are actively lining up options.

## One-Pager Leave-Behind PDF Concept
This should become a simple print-friendly handout or PDF for hotels, offices, studios, and workshop hosts.

### One-pager structure
- Headline: “A group lunch people actually remember.”
- Subhead: Local, custom-friendly pizza for workshops, offices, guests, and group events
- 3 bullets on why choose Fork in the Road
- A few real-photo visuals
- Contact information
- Short note that requests are welcome

### Sample bullet set
- More memorable than standard sandwich chains
- Flexible for groups and custom requests
- Local Portland spot with personality

## Word-of-Mouth Strategy
The strongest marketing engine is making the place easy to describe and recommend.

### Build word-of-mouth by making these things obvious
- The owner takes requests
- The pizzas are distinctive
- The shop feels human and local
- It is a good answer to “Where should we order lunch from?”

### Customer language to reinforce
- “You should try this place.”
- “They’ll actually make interesting stuff.”
- “It’s better than the usual office lunch.”
- “It feels local, not corporate.”

## Priority Actions for the Next 30 Days

### Week 1
- Optimize Google Business Profile fully
- Gather the best existing customer photos
- Identify 10 local hotels, co-working spaces, studios, and offices to approach

### Week 2
- Start posting request-based pizza content on Instagram
- Draft and print a simple hotel/business one-pager
- Begin direct outreach to the first 10 target partners

### Week 3
- Ask happy customers for reviews consistently
- Track which outreach messages get replies
- Refine talking points based on what people respond to

### Week 4
- Double down on the best-performing outreach targets
- Refresh Google photos
- Pick one recurring content theme to keep posting consistently

## What Not to Do
- Do not try to be on every social platform
- Do not spend heavily on ads before the messaging is proven
- Do not create a complicated brand system before basic outreach is working
- Do not overextend into complicated catering operations too early
- Do not market like a generic pizza shop if the real advantage is uniqueness

## Success Metrics
Track a few simple things each week:
- Number of new Google reviews
- Number of hotel or partner conversations started
- Number of repeat group or office orders
- Instagram posts that drive comments, shares, or direct inquiries
- Number of customer photos or stories worth reposting

## Final Direction
Fork in the Road should market through proof, personality, and partnerships. The winning move is to show real pizzas, tell real stories, and make it easy for hotels, offices, and workshop hosts to choose a lunch option that feels more alive than the default.
